Fat Free Mass Index (FFMI)

The Fat-free mass index (FFMI) is proposed to analyze body composition among individuals with identical body composition but different heights, allowing identification of those suffering from malnutrition, wasting, or having a comparatively high muscle mass. A lean mass ratio is calculated by taking a person's body fat percentage, height, and weight into account.
Simply said, the Fat-free mass index (FFMI) is a measurement of your lean mass (muscularity) in relation to your height. There are two steps to calculating your FFMI... provided you know your body fat percentage. Calculate your lean weight (in kilograms) as follows:

Lean Weight = Total Weight * (100% - Body Fat) (kg)

Lean Weight (kg) / (Height (m) * Height (m)) = FFMI.

Back then, FFMI was utilized to determine whether a person was natural or used steroids. The idea that an FFMI of 25 is the "natty limit for males" can be traced back to this study: "Fat-Free Mass in Anabolic-Androgenic Steroid Users and Nonusers" by Kouri, 1995. However, if you accept the statistics at its value, it appears that not many people would pass an FFMI of 25 without the use of steroids, but that people with exceptional genetics can obtain FFMIs of 26-27+. However, anyone with an FFMI of 28 or above for men and 23 or higher for women is not usually considered natural.

In reality, I believe that defining a "limit" is misguided in the first place, because human features are regularly distributed and exception genes exist.
